About Me

I am a PhD candidate in Biomedical Sciences at the East Tennessee State University Quillen College of Medicine. My research focuses on analyzing drug abuse vulnerability in psychosis via neurobiological mechanisms in a heritable rodent model of dopamine dysregulation. My work has previously focused on modulation and stimulation of the mutually inhibitory adenosine A(2A)-metabotropic glutamate receptor type 5 (mGlu5)-dopamine D2 heteroreceptor complex.

Our laboratory has completed fiber photometry live dopamine imaging in the nucleus accumbens shell to evaluate differences in reward response between the offspring of quinpirole-treated animals and controls. Additionally, we are evaluating susceptibility to drug relapse in our model via direct exposure to nicotine or stressful stimuli.

The primary focus of my dissertation is evaluating the role of α4β2 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs) in a nicotine vapor conditioned place preference paradigm and its impact on neural plasticity markers in our heritable model of dopamine dysregulation. The overarching goal of this project is to elucidate novel mechanistic actions and treatment targets aimed at reducing behavioral deficits in psychosis while simultaneously reducing the rewarding effects of comorbid nicotine use.
